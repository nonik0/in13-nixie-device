# IN13 Nixie Device

## Overview

This Nixie project is a continutation of the same spirit of most of my earlier Nixie projects: take an existing Nixie device, house it in a clean, well-built metal case, and maybe do a few addtions or modifications along the way. For me, the magic of Nixie tubes lies in their soft, ghostly glow—subtle, warm, and inviting. A simple, high-quality enclosure helps keep the attention where it belongs: on the Nixie itself.

Over the years, I’ve owned a few different Nixie thermometers, but they all came in acrylic cases. Aside from being acrylic, they were also bulky compared to the slim IN-13 tube and also used LED edge-lit acrylic to indicate temperature ranges. I don’t hate edge-lit acrylic on principle, but pairing that aesthetic with the aesthetic of Nixie tubes feels like a cardinal sin to me. I know there’s a huge market for flashy Nixie clocks with rainbow LEDs and effects, and plenty of people enjoy that style, but it’s just not for me. In my house, there’s a Nixie device in every room—and not a single RGB LED in sight. That’s been a personal rule for a while now.

At some point while browsing other Nixie projects, my mental levee broke and I had to start this project. TODO prototype? I started by finding a case I liked and sized well with an IN-13 tube, and worked back from that. My challenge for this was creating relatively cramped single sided PCB custom made for the case I had chosen, and this project is the result! and I dove into building this. I started by picking out a metal case I liked that fit the IN-13 well, and worked backward from there. My main challenge for this project was designing a compact, single-sided PCB tailored specifically to the dimensions of the enclosure I had chosen, and what you see here is the result!

## Hardware
The hardware is designed in KiCad 8.0, and all design files are included in the [hardware/kicad8.0](hardware/kicad8.0) directory.

- IN-13 Nixie Tube
